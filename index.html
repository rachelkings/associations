We ate sushi on our last night alone, together, as a family, just my parents and I. Where were we that night? I can't really remember. A quiet rest stop off the highway. Somewhere outside Victoria, maybe, on a bench overlooking the ocean. Someone had left their dog tied to their truck bed and the dog kept trying to jump down and I couldn't stop imagining it hanging from its leash or, forgotten, dragged along the dirt road by a careless driver. Nothing bad happened. We were alone, and we were together. It wasn't about happiness (although we were happy) but rather the bittersweetness of our last night on the west coast alone/together. We'd spent the first couple nights with family, before embarking upon our own journey up the coast, but now we were returning to spend the last night of our trip with them. (We'd planned the trip before staying with them, so we hadn't really had any way of knowing that we'd barely be able to stand another minute with them. These were my thoughts, at least. I'm horribly incapable of hiding my feelings, and my parents are the exact opposite.)

We shared a room for two nights in Montréal. The sweltering Quebec summer was worsened by the lack of air conditioning and the inescapable scent of cigarette smoke left by our hosts. The constant discomfort, the feeling of wanting to be as far apart as possible in the same bed (and hadn't we been so excited to fall asleep together?) culminated in a near-constant haze of discomfort. I loved Montreal, though. I loved the old university buildings and the breakfast restaurants that made you forget that you weren't in Europe. I guess we'd already started falling out of love before that trip, but I'd convinced myself that everything would fix itself if we went away together. I remember on the first day, I offered to take pictures of these girls, and after, they asked if you and I wanted a picture together, and I reflexively declined. I knew that it would be a spectacle and you'd be mad at me for making you interact with strangers and I didn't have to even process that before saying no. I guess I realized that everything that had become natural wasn't making me happy. I wasn't making you happy, either. But I want to go back to Montréal. I want to go to the jazz club that we decided to avoid. I want to spend more time at the art gallery without having to find you because you wandered off, bored, not answering your phone.
